# About Page Concepts: B and C

---

## Concept B: "Scrolling through Flo's messages"

**The idea:** The About page looks like one side of a conversation. Just your messages, as if someone opened a chat with you and scrolled up. No other person visible. The reader becomes the person on the other end.

**Visual format:**
- Blue/green message bubbles aligned to the right (like iMessage "sent" side)
- Timestamps between clusters (subtle, like "Today", "Yesterday", "March 2020")
- Delivered/read receipts at the bottom of some clusters
- Occasional image bubbles (the emoji physics animation, a screenshot of Apple featuring SpotAngels, a photo of you surfing)
- Maybe one voice message bubble (just the waveform, not playable - hints at personality)
- Typing indicator "..." at the very bottom of the page (you're still writing your story)

**The content, as a message thread:**

---

[Today]

I've been designing message bubbles for a while now.

First I made them safe. I designed systems that stop the worst from happening before someone has to live through it.

That team taught me something. The most important design work is the kind nobody ever sees.

[image: blurred/abstract safety UI]

Then I moved to media. People's content deserves to look better in a chat than in their camera roll.

We once spent weeks building a physics engine just for an emoji reaction animation.

[image: the emoji physics animation]

Small thing. But it's what made people care about the entire project.

Craft isn't decoration. It's how you get people to pay attention.

Now I'm at MSL. Robots are entering the chat, and I'm still figuring out what that should feel like.

[Yesterday]

I got into design the unusual way. French literature at the Sorbonne, then product design at EnsAD in Paris.

My first job in SF was at SpotAngels, a tiny parking app. Came back as their only designer. Apple featured us three times in one year.

My parents were very proud, which is the only metric that matters.

[image: App Store feature or something personal]

I build to think. SwiftUI, Next.js, Claude Code. The best way to explain an idea is to let someone touch it.

I also teach designers to ship production code at Meta. We call it "Designers Ship Diffs."

[Earlier]

I live in France with Emma (also a designer, we argue about spacing at dinner) and our daughter Michelle.

I surf when there are waves, hike when there aren't.

I occasionally build AI agents that sell our old clothes online.

[voice message waveform: 0:04]

[typing indicator ...]

---

**Why this works:**
- The format is the brand. You design message bubbles. Your bio IS message bubbles.
- It's intimate. Reading someone's messages feels personal, like you're getting access to how they actually talk.
- Short messages force tight writing. No room for resume bloat.
- Images feel native (you share photos in chats all the time).
- The typing indicator at the bottom says "the story isn't over." It's a subtle, beautiful detail.
- Nobody else's portfolio looks like this.

**Risks:**
- Could feel gimmicky if the visual execution isn't perfect.
- Accessibility needs thought (screen readers need to read it as content, not decoration).
- Mobile needs to feel native, not like a fake iMessage screenshot.

## Concept C: "So what do you actually do?"

**The idea:** A real conversation between you and a friend. Two sides of bubbles. The friend asks casual questions, you answer. It feels like eavesdropping on a real chat.

**Visual format:**
- Two-sided chat: friend on the left (gray), you on the right (blue)
- Friend's messages are short, casual, curious
- Your messages are the actual bio content
- Occasional reactions (the friend dropping a 😮 or ❤️ on one of your messages)
- The friend's questions guide the reader through your story naturally

**The content, as a conversation:**

---

**Friend:** wait so what do you actually do at meta

**You:** I design message bubbles

**Friend:** like the blue ones?

**You:** those ones yes
I've been doing it for a while now

**Friend:** what does that even mean though

**You:** it started with safety. I designed systems that stop the worst from happening before someone has to live through it.

**Friend:** damn

**You:** yeah. that team taught me that the best design work is the kind nobody ever sees.

then I moved to media. crafting how people's photos and videos show up in conversations. their content deserves to look better in a chat than in their camera roll.

**Friend:** that's a cool way to think about it

**You:** we once spent weeks building a physics engine just for an emoji reaction

**Friend:** weeks???

**You:** small thing. but it's what made leadership care about the entire project.
craft isn't decoration. it's how you get people to pay attention.

**Friend:** so what's the new thing

**You:** MSL. robots are entering the chat.

**Friend:** lol

**You:** seriously though. I'm still figuring out what that should feel like.

**Friend:** ok back up. how did you even get into design

**You:** french literature at the Sorbonne

**Friend:** ???

**You:** then product design at EnsAD in Paris. first job in SF was at SpotAngels, a tiny parking app. came back as their only designer. Apple featured us three times in one year.

my parents were very proud, which is the only metric that matters.

[Friend reacted ❤️ to "my parents were very proud, which is the only metric that matters."]

**Friend:** do you code too or just design

**You:** I build to think. SwiftUI, Next.js, Claude Code. I think the best way to explain an idea is to let someone touch it.

**Friend:** what about outside work

**You:** I live in France with Emma, also a designer. we argue about spacing at dinner.

**Friend:** of course you do

[typing indicator ...]

---

**Why this works:**
- Feels like a real conversation. The friend's reactions ("damn", "weeks???", "???") create rhythm and humor.
- The questions guide the reader naturally. No awkward transitions between topics.
- Message reactions (❤️ on the parents line) add emotion without words.
- The friend's personality makes Flo's answers feel more human by contrast.
- The "of course you do" at the end is the perfect closer.
- Still nobody else's portfolio looks like this.

**Risks:**
- The friend character needs to feel real, not like a convenient interviewer. If the questions feel scripted, the whole thing falls apart.
- Longer than B. More content to get right.
- Two-column chat on mobile needs careful responsive design.
- Who is the friend? Does it matter? (I'd say no, keep them anonymous. It's more universal.)

---

## My take

**B is more elegant.** It's your monologue, your voice, nothing diluting it. The images and typing indicator do the emotional work. It's art-directed.

**C is more alive.** The friend adds warmth, humor, and pacing that's hard to achieve alone. The reactions and short replies ("damn", "???") create moments that B can't. It's storytelling.

If I had to pick: **C is more Flo.** The warmth, the humor, the back-and-forth is exactly how you communicate. And it's a harder format to pull off, which means when it works, it signals more craft.

But B is the safer bet if you want something that ages well and doesn't depend on getting the friend's voice right.
