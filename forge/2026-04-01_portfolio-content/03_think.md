# Think: Brand Positioning Approaches

## Problem Reference
The site's tone and humor work. What's missing is a brand - a coherent story about what kind of designer Flo is, what he believes, and why his work matters. Every detail (domain, tagline, descriptions, about, meta) should be an expression of that brand.

## What makes Flo distinct?

Before approaches, let me map the raw material - the things that are actually true and distinctive about Flo's career:

**The unusual path:** French literature at the Sorbonne -> design school -> tiny parking app in SF -> Apple features -> Meta safety systems -> Messenger/Instagram media -> MSL AI agents. This isn't a linear career. It's someone who keeps moving toward harder, less-defined problems.

**The builder identity:** Prototypes in SwiftUI, Next.js, Claude Code. "Designers Ship Diffs" initiative. Built an AI agent that sells clothes. Built a full dashboard system then deleted it. This person doesn't just design - he makes things real.

**The thinker identity:** Writes about taste, about AI's limits, about what happens when building gets too easy. Questions his own tools. Deleted his own dashboard. Has opinions about craft and quality that push back against the AI hype.

**The craft obsession:** "The details most people don't notice." The extra mile. The difference between generating something and knowing if it's good.

**The range:** Safety systems protecting people from scams -> media sharing -> AI agents. Government internships -> startup -> Big Tech. French literature -> product design. This person operates across contexts.

**The human element:** Non-native English speaker leading in English. Lives in France, works for a US tech giant. Family person. Surfer. Humor in everything.

---

## Approach 1: "The Builder Who Thinks"

**Core idea:** Position Flo at the intersection of making and thinking. Most senior designers either become pure strategists (they stop building) or stay as craftspeople (they stop thinking at the systems level). Flo does both - he builds prototypes with code AND writes essays questioning his own tools.

**Brand line direction:** Something like "I design by building and I build by thinking."

**Through-line across work:**
- AI taste essay = thinking about what craft means in the AI age
- OpenClaw = building an actual system to test AI's limits
- Dashboard essay = building something, then thinking critically enough to kill it
- Safety work = building systems that protect people
- Designers Ship Diffs = teaching other designers to build

**Domain ideas:** Could lean into the build/think duality. Or simply use the name with authority.

**Risk:** "Builder" is overused in tech. Could feel like another "I ship code" designer trying to prove engineering cred.

---

## Approach 2: "Design at the Edges"

**Core idea:** Flo's career is defined by working on problems that don't have established playbooks. Safety systems before "trust and safety" was a standard team. AI agents before anyone knows what AI agent UX should look like. A parking app that needed to convince Apple. Every role has been at the frontier of something.

**Brand line direction:** Something about designing in spaces where the patterns don't exist yet.

**Through-line across work:**
- AI agents at MSL = literally inventing the interaction patterns
- AI taste essay = defining what quality means when the tools are new
- Safety systems = designing protections before the playbook existed
- OpenClaw = exploring what AI agents can actually do in the real world
- Dashboard essay = discovering the limits of a new paradigm by hitting them

**Domain ideas:** Something that suggests frontier, edges, uncharted.

**Risk:** Could sound grandiose. "I work on the hard problems" can come across as self-important if not grounded.

---

## Approach 3: "Craft in the Age of AI"

**Core idea:** The world is flooding with AI-generated everything. Flo's positioning is that craft, taste, and critical thinking matter MORE now, not less. He's not anti-AI (he builds with it constantly), but he believes the human layer - judgment, taste, knowing when to delete - is what separates good from noise.

**Brand line direction:** Something about the human judgment layer. What stays when everything can be generated.

**Through-line across work:**
- AI taste essay = the thesis statement of this brand
- Dashboard essay = proof he practices what he preaches (built it, judged it, killed it)
- OpenClaw = using AI as a tool, not worshipping it
- Designers Ship Diffs = craft means getting close to the material
- Safety work = protecting the human experience in digital products

**Domain ideas:** Something that signals craft, intentionality, or the human layer.

**Risk:** Could position Flo as primarily an "AI + design" person when his career is broader than that. Also, "craft" is a word every designer claims.

---

## Approach 4: "The Honest Designer"

**Core idea:** What's most distinctive about Flo's writing is the honesty. He wrote about deleting his own work. He writes about "the things I get wrong." He questions AI while building with it. In a world of designer portfolios that only show wins and polished case studies, Flo shows the thinking - including the doubt, the wrong turns, and the deletions.

**Brand line direction:** Something about showing the real process, not just the output.

**Through-line across work:**
- Dashboard essay = "I built something impressive and it was useless"
- AI taste essay = "everyone can design now and most of it isn't good, including mine"
- OpenClaw = "here's exactly what happened, including the parts I didn't expect"
- Older articles = customer support duty, asking the president, design principles as self-correction

**Domain ideas:** Something direct, unpretentious.

**Risk:** "Honest" can read as "junior." Senior designers are expected to have conviction. Too much self-doubt becomes a brand problem, not a brand asset. This is actually the current site's issue - the honesty is there but the authority isn't.

---

## Recommendation: Blend of 1 + 3, grounded by 4's honesty

**The brand should be: A designer who builds to think, and thinks to build - with a specific belief that craft and judgment matter more in the AI age, not less.**

Here's why:

- **Approach 1** (builder who thinks) gives the structural identity - it's what makes Flo different from pure strategists or pure craftspeople. But it needs a belief system to have weight.
- **Approach 3** (craft in the age of AI) provides that belief system - it's the lens through which all the work connects. But on its own it's too narrow (Flo's career pre-dates AI).
- **Approach 4's honesty** is the tone layer - it's already working on the site and should stay. But it can't be the brand itself because honesty without authority reads as uncertainty.
- **Approach 2** (edges) is true but too abstract to build a brand around. It's a supporting detail, not the headline.

**Combined positioning:**
> Flo is a design leader who works at the intersection of building and thinking. He builds prototypes, ships code, and makes AI agents do real things. He also writes critically about the tools he uses, questions the work he ships, and cares about the craft details that most people skip. His career has always moved toward the problems without playbooks - safety, media, AI agents - and his belief is that human judgment is the thing that makes any of it good.

**This gives us:**
- A homepage intro that leads with a POV, not a job title
- An About page organized by *beliefs and approach*, not chronology
- Article descriptions that connect each piece to the larger brand
- A meta description that signals what makes this person worth following
- Domain name options that reinforce the identity

## Killed Ideas
- "Design leadership thought leader" angle - too LinkedIn, too abstract, Flo makes things
- Pure "AI designer" positioning - too narrow, career is broader
- "French designer in tech" angle - interesting color but not a brand
- Portfolio with case studies approach - Flo chose writing-first for a reason, lean into that
